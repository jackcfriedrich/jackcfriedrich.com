Intro

I'm an organizational researcher and data scientist finishing a PhD in Industrial-Organizational Psychology at Saint Louis University (expected May 2026). My work spans two tracks I try to keep in conversation with each other: doing rigorous science and putting it to use.

On the research side, I study the conditions under which science goes well and goes wrong — how researchers make decisions about their methods, what incentives shape those choices, and how open science practices can improve both the credibility and the reach of organizational research. My dissertation focuses on questionable research practices in the organizational sciences. I've published nine peer-reviewed articles, including a co-authored piece in Science.

On the applied side, I build things. At Nestlé Purina, I develop time-series forecasting models, Power BI dashboards, and survey infrastructure. In consulting, I've built leadership measures, evaluated mentoring programs, and facilitated DEI workshops. In the lab, I've run meta-analyses, fielded international surveys, and helped lead an open science journal club.

I'm relocating to Bern, Switzerland in May 2026 — Swiss B permit via family reunification. I'm open to research collaborations, consulting engagements, and full-time roles in data science or applied organizational research.

Research

My dissertation — and the thread running through most of my research career — is about how science goes wrong even when researchers are trying to do it right.

The Question: How do I-O psychologists and management researchers perceive the severity of questionable research practices, and what drives those perceptions? Are some practices seen as more acceptable than others, and why?

What I Did: Designed and fielded an international survey of I-O psychology and management faculty. Analyzed perceptions of QRP severity using regression and relative weights analysis. Led the SLU chapter of ReproducibiliTea and co-authored work on bridging the science-practice gap.

Impact: Presenting at SIOP Annual Conference, New Orleans, 2026. Funded by the Dustin Jundt Legacy Fund and Knoedler Student Research Funds, Saint Louis University. Related work published in The Industrial-Organizational Psychologist and presented at SIOP 2023 and 2024.

A perspective piece in Science co-authored with an international team of researchers across metascience, psychology, and open science.

The Question: How should scientific teams handle internal disagreement — and what does scientific culture get wrong about consensus?

What We Argued: Productive disagreement is a feature, not a bug, of good team science. We outlined norms for how research teams can surface and engage internal dissent rather than suppress it in pursuit of a unified narrative.

Impact: Coles, N. A., Tenney, E. R., Chin, J. M., Friedrich, J. C., O'Dea, R. E., & Holcombe, A. O. (2024). Science, 384(6700), 1076–1077.

A quantitative synthesis of the empirical literature on character strengths use at work and its relations to job performance and employee wellbeing.

The Question: Does deploying your character strengths at work actually improve outcomes — and if so, which outcomes, and for whom?

What I Did: Co-led the meta-analysis: conducted literature searches, extracted and coded effect sizes, and analyzed data using meta-analytic techniques in R.

Impact: Rudolph, C. W., Friedrich, J. C., Koziel, R. J., & Zacher, H. (2025). Applied Research in Quality of Life. Also presented at EAWOP 2023 (Katowice, Poland).

An empirical study testing a mediation model of work engagement, conducted during the early COVID-19 pandemic.

The Question: Why do some employees stay engaged at work under conditions of resource depletion — and does work ability explain that relationship?

What I Did: Co-led data collection and analysis. Built and tested mediation models examining personal resources → work ability → work engagement using regression-based approaches in R.

Impact: Friedrich, J. C., Koziel, R. J., Zacher, H., & Rudolph, C. W. (2022). Merits, 2(4). Presented at SIOP 2022, Seattle.

A psychometric scale development study in partnership with SLU School of Medicine, building on needs assessment work conducted in consulting.

The Question: What behaviours distinguish effective mentees in workplace mentoring partnerships, and can we measure them reliably?

What I Did: Co-developed the item pool grounded in mentoring theory, piloted and refined the scale, and conducted psychometric analyses including factor analysis and validity assessment.

Impact: Giancola, J. K., Webster, I. D., Friedrich, J. C., & Burroughs, T. E. (2025). International Journal of Evidence Based Coaching and Mentoring, 23(1), 284–299.
